Business & Professions Code § 3765

Foundation Statute

What It Does

This statute establishes the foundational definition and scope of respiratory care practice in California. It serves as the legal basis for all respiratory care regulation in the state.

Scope of Practice Defined

  • Respiratory care practitioner (RCP): A person licensed to practice respiratory care under the Respiratory Care Practice Act
  • Respiratory care: Treatment, management, diagnostic evaluation, and care of patients with deficiencies and abnormalities of the cardiopulmonary system
  • Includes administration of pharmacologic agents, oxygen, and other therapeutic gases
  • Encompasses mechanical ventilation, bronchopulmonary hygiene, and cardiopulmonary resuscitation
  • Allows maintenance of natural and artificial airways
Key Principle: BPC § 3765 establishes that respiratory care is a licensed profession, but it does not prohibit all non-RCPs from performing certain basic respiratory tasks. This created the legal foundation for subsequent regulations and legislation.

Who It Applies To

This statute governs Respiratory Care Practitioners and establishes the Respiratory Care Board of California's authority to regulate the profession.

Detailed Legal Language ▼

BPC § 3765(a) — "Respiratory care practitioner" means a person licensed under this chapter to practice respiratory care.

BPC § 3765(b) — "Respiratory care" means the treatment, management, diagnostic evaluation, and care of patients with deficiencies and abnormalities of the cardiopulmonary system and associated aspects of other systems. The practice includes:

  • Therapeutic and life support services
  • Pulmonary rehabilitation
  • Patient assessment and testing
  • Implementation of respiratory care protocols

Title 16 CCR §§ 1399.361–1399.365

Regulatory Framework Effective Oct 1, 2025

What This Regulation Does

These regulations, adopted by the Respiratory Care Board of California, define "basic respiratory tasks" that may be performed by non-RCPs (including LVNs) without violating the RCP licensing requirements.

Operative Date: October 1, 2025 — This is when the regulation officially took effect, creating a clear legal framework for non-RCP performance of basic respiratory tasks.

Key Sections

§ 1399.365 — Basic Respiratory Tasks Defined ▼

What Tasks Are Included:

  • Oxygen therapy: Administration via nasal cannula, simple face mask, or non-rebreather mask
  • Aerosol therapy: Nebulized medications administered via small volume nebulizer
  • Pulse oximetry: Measurement and monitoring of oxygen saturation
  • Suctioning: Oral and nasopharyngeal suctioning (NOT tracheal)
Critical Limitations:
  • Does NOT include mechanical ventilation
  • Does NOT include tracheal suctioning
  • Does NOT include CPAP/BiPAP initiation
  • Does NOT include arterial blood gas sampling
  • Does NOT include chest physiotherapy

Requirements for Non-RCPs Performing These Tasks:

  • Must have valid order from physician or RCP
  • Must be within their professional scope (e.g., LVN scope)
  • Must have appropriate training and competency verification
  • Must work under appropriate supervision
§§ 1399.361-1399.364 — Related Provisions ▼

These sections provide additional context and requirements:

  • § 1399.361: Definitions and scope
  • § 1399.362: Training requirements
  • § 1399.363: Supervision standards
  • § 1399.364: Documentation requirements

Why This Matters

Before these regulations, there was significant ambiguity about what respiratory tasks non-RCPs could legally perform. The regulation provides clarity and legal protection for healthcare facilities, LVNs, and patients.

Senate Bill 1451 (2024)

LVN Authorization Home Health

What This Law Does

SB 1451, signed into law in 2024, specifically authorizes Licensed Vocational Nurses (LVNs) to perform basic respiratory care tasks in home health settings.

Problem Solved: Before SB 1451, LVNs working in home health faced legal uncertainty about performing respiratory tasks, even basic ones like oxygen administration. This law provided explicit authorization.

Key Provisions

  • Authorization: LVNs may perform basic respiratory tasks as defined in Title 16 CCR § 1399.365
  • Setting: Home health and hospice settings only
  • Requirements: LVNs must act under physician order or RCP supervision
  • Training: LVNs must receive appropriate training and demonstrate competency
  • Scope limitation: Only "basic" tasks — no ventilator management or advanced procedures

Impact on Practice

Before SB 1451 After SB 1451
Legal uncertainty for LVNs performing respiratory tasks in home health Clear legal authorization for basic respiratory tasks
Many home health agencies reluctant to allow LVNs to provide respiratory care LVNs can confidently provide oxygen therapy, nebulizers, suctioning, pulse oximetry
Patients with respiratory needs often required RN visits (more expensive) Cost-effective care delivery with LVN services
Potential gaps in care for home health patients Improved access to respiratory care at home
Legislative Intent & Background ▼

Why SB 1451 Was Needed:

  • Growing elderly population with chronic respiratory conditions
  • Shortage of RCPs and RNs in home health settings
  • Need for cost-effective care delivery models
  • LVNs have training and capability to safely perform basic respiratory tasks
  • Existing legal ambiguity created barriers to care

Legislative Process:

  • Introduced in 2024 legislative session
  • Supported by California Association for Health Services at Home
  • Passed Assembly and Senate
  • Signed by Governor in 2024
  • Works in conjunction with CCR § 1399.365 (operative Oct 1, 2025)

Senate Bill 389 (2025)

School Setting Unlicensed Personnel

What This Law Does

SB 389, signed into law in 2025, creates a narrow, school-specific exemption allowing trained unlicensed school personnel to administer aerosol medications to students.

Narrow Scope: SB 389 is highly specific — it only applies to K-12 schools and only covers aerosol medication administration (nebulizers). It does not expand general respiratory care authority.

Key Provisions

  • Who: Unlicensed school personnel (not required to be RCPs, RNs, or LVNs)
  • What: Aerosol medication administration via nebulizer only
  • Where: K-12 public and private schools only
  • Requirements: Training, competency verification, and authorization from school nurse
  • Student consent: Parent/guardian must provide written authorization
  • Medical order: Valid physician order required for the student

Why This Law Was Needed

Problem: Many students with asthma need nebulizer treatments during school day
↓
Barrier: Not enough school nurses to administer all treatments; RCP requirement impractical
↓
Result: Students missed treatments or parents had to leave work to administer medications
↓
Solution: SB 389 allows trained school staff to administer nebulizers under nurse oversight

Strict Limitations

What SB 389 Does NOT Allow:
  • Does NOT apply outside school settings
  • Does NOT allow oxygen administration
  • Does NOT allow suctioning
  • Does NOT allow any ventilator or CPAP/BiPAP management
  • Does NOT eliminate need for school nurse oversight
  • Does NOT allow administration without valid physician order
Training & Safety Requirements ▼

Required Training Components:

  • Proper nebulizer assembly and operation
  • Medication administration technique
  • Recognition of adverse reactions
  • Emergency response procedures
  • Documentation requirements
  • Infection control practices

Ongoing Requirements:

  • School nurse must verify competency before authorization
  • Regular refresher training required
  • Documentation of each administration
  • Communication with parents and school nurse
  • Annual review of authorization
Relationship to Other Laws ▼

SB 389 works within the existing regulatory framework:

  • BPC § 3765: SB 389 creates a specific exemption to RCP licensing for this narrow school-based activity
  • CCR § 1399.365: Aerosol administration is a "basic respiratory task," but SB 389 goes further by allowing unlicensed personnel in schools
  • SB 1451: Different scope (home health vs. schools) and different personnel (LVNs vs. unlicensed)
Important Distinction: SB 389 is a carve-out specifically for schools. It does not set a general precedent for unlicensed respiratory care — it's a limited exception based on the unique needs of the school environment.

How These Laws & Regulations Work Together

Understanding the Hierarchy: California's respiratory care regulatory framework consists of a foundational statute (BPC § 3765), implementing regulations (CCR), and specific legislative authorizations (SB 1451 & SB 389) that work together to define who can do what, where, and how.

Key Relationships

BPC § 3765 ← → Title 16 CCR ▼

Relationship: The Respiratory Care Board has regulatory authority under BPC § 3765 to adopt regulations that implement and clarify the statute.

  • BPC § 3765 establishes: General scope of respiratory care practice and RCP licensing requirements
  • CCR implements by: Defining specific "basic respiratory tasks" that fall within nursing or other healthcare professional scopes
  • Result: CCR § 1399.365 clarifies which respiratory tasks are "basic" enough that they don't require RCP licensure when performed by other licensed professionals
Key Insight: BPC § 3765 doesn't prohibit ALL non-RCPs from doing ANY respiratory care. The CCR regulations clarify that certain basic tasks can be performed by other healthcare professionals within their scopes of practice.
Title 16 CCR ← → SB 1451 ▼

Relationship: SB 1451 explicitly references and relies on the CCR definition of "basic respiratory tasks."

  • CCR § 1399.365 defines: What constitutes "basic respiratory tasks"
  • SB 1451 authorizes: LVNs to perform those specific tasks in home health settings
  • Timing: SB 1451 passed in 2024, CCR became operative Oct 1, 2025 — they work together
Interdependence: Without the CCR definition, SB 1451 would be vague. Without SB 1451, LVNs might still face legal uncertainty in home health despite the CCR. Together, they provide comprehensive clarity.
SB 1451 vs. SB 389: Different Contexts ▼

Relationship: These bills address different settings and different personnel — they don't conflict, they complement.

Where We Are Today: California now has a comprehensive, clear regulatory framework governing respiratory care practice. The combination of BPC § 3765, Title 16 CCR §§ 1399.361–1399.365, SB 1451, and SB 389 provides legal certainty for healthcare providers, protects patient safety, and improves access to respiratory care across multiple settings.
